Digital Effects on Perceived Wellness:
A Look at the Effect of Increased Gaming on Quality of Life During the Pandemic.
A project for UArizona iSchools Online Qualitative Research course
by: Nicholas Skophammer May 4th 2022

╔ INTRODUCTION ╗

During the Covid-19 pandemic many businesses were forced to close, lay off employees or adjust hours to maintain their bottom line, but one of the industries that showed positive growth during the Pandemic was the video game industry. For many people videogames are a fun, interactive way to pass the time, for other’s they’re barely an afterthought. Even before the Pandemic people would use video games as a meeting place, or the forums of a game developers’ website to have conversations. It’s no wonder that when everything was shut down, that video games became a popular pastime, even to those who had not played much previously.

Introduction to the Pilot Research Study:

Was this because people were bored and had more free time, or did video games provide more than just entertainment? My close friends and I are all avid gamers, yet we each take something different away from the experience. For some of us, it is just an entertaining way to pass the time, for a few of us, it's an escape from reality and a coping mechanism. Others use it as a medium through which to socialize. And one or two of us just couldn't identify ourselves without including "gamer" in the description because they provide a deeper meaning to our lives. If there is such diversity in value amongst 11 friends, it would be valuable to know as it pertains to the population in general. Going into the Game Design and Development field, understanding the motivations and impact video games can have on people is paramount, if one wants to be successful in the field. So this will affect me directly and the knowledge would benefit other game developers as well.

Underlying Framework

Due to the nature of the question and videogames themselves, online seemed to be the obvious place to begin when researching this question. Games are usually played online if played with other people. Using an Exploratory Qualitative approach would “maximize the discovery of generalizations leading to a detailed and profound understanding of the group, process, or activity under study” (Stebbins, 2001, p. 3).

Quantitative study focusing on the well-being of the individual as it relates to the increase in video game habits. The study found a positive correlation between a person's mental well-being and the increase in video game habits during the Pandemic. Survey emphasizes the limitations of the study being that the well-being of participants was reported, which is valid but not the full picture because it did not measure or take into account their physical well-being.


Donati MA, Guido CA, De Meo G, Spalice A, Sanson F, Beccari C, Primi C. Gaming among Children and Adolescents during the COVID-19 Lockdown: The Role of Parents in Time Spent on Video Games and Gaming Disorder Symptoms. Int J Environ Res Public Health. 2021 Jun 21;18(12):6642. doi: 10.3390/ijerph18126642. PMID: 34205533; PMCID: PMC8296495.


This study found a positive correlation between children with gaming disorder symptoms and parents who played video games regularly. Delves into the psychology behind gaming disorders and concludes that strict monitoring of children and game play habits be maintained during the pandemic to prevent further increase in detrimental habits


Marston HR and Kowert R. What role can videogames play in the COVID-19 pandemic? [version 1; peer review: 2 approved]. Emerald Open Res 2020, 2:34 (https://doi.org/10.35241/emeraldopenres.13727.1)

This article examines the role of video games prior to the Pandemic and their effect specifically on adults aging without children and their mental well-being, as well as touching on the effects of gaming on people with PTSD and other traumas. It also touches on how video games even before the pandemic were a way through which the older generations were able to connect with their grandchildren, which, is a microcosm of one of the positive observations noted in my study, in which participants often emphasized the ability of video games to make them feel connected and not so socially isolated.

╔ Reflection and Ethics ╗

The topic of video games and mental health hits home on a multiplicity of fronts for me. It was imperative therefore for me to constantly examine and re-examine my observations and evaluations to be as unbiased a researcher as possible. I am fully aware that eliminating all bias is impossible, but a relative level of self-actualization and familiarity with my own thought processes and normative deficiencies in perception or proper knowledge was critical in identifying detrimental bias’.


Roaming through Reddit, Steam and Discord, an important aspect of ethics that I was sure to follow was maintaining individual privacy and ensuring that discourse areas were open to the public and that personally protected information was not compromised or even collected.


When performing the interview, I ensured that the participant signed the informed consent paperwork after having him explain back to me its meaning and was sure he had full understanding of the use of the interview and its purpose. He signed the Multi-media release and consent form with the understanding that it was not going to be included in the content displayed on the webpage and was just for administrative purposes and to ensure that he was ok with the recording of our interview being displayed on the webpage.

╔ Methods of Data Collection ╗

Extant Data Methods

Any extant methods consisted primarily of browsing public forums that commonly engage in discourse about a variety of topics and channels that engaged in commentary and debate over video games and topics relating directly to video games.

Elicited Data Methods

For the elicited data I choose to focus primarily on an interview with a particular subject. This allowed me to delve more deeply into the topic. I choose a more relaxed and informal style of interview to promote an environment of tranquility and trust that would allow the interviewee the comfort to open up more fully and express their feelings without the extra anxiety and stress of a more formal one-on-one interview.

╔ Data Analysis ╗

Corpus Description and Excerpts

The sites, broadly speaking, were Reddit, Discord, Steam Community, and the Bethesda Softworks forums. When searching reddit, I mainly stuck to subreddits pertaining to videogames or mental health as their primary focus. INTERVIEW AUDIO

How the Data was Analyzed

Once I had all the elicited and extant data, I used a few different qualitative research methods to analyze it all. One such method was codeweaving in which I tried to piece together the data like puzzle pieces. The data that I found via the various methods seem to point to Twitch being a place that was conducive to relationships forming. The real-time interactions closely mimicked interactions that would take place in real life. These circumstances proved to be unique to the participants and seem to align with my initial hypothesis about how interpersonal relationships can form organically in a space like Twitch. “Deduction is reaching a conclusion based on an analysis of evidence” (Saldana 23). Having the expectation of what to expect and deducing what will come next is how I approached data analysis and it seemed to work out.

╔ Findings and Conclusion ╗

Findings and Conclusion

Just over half of the individuals making comments on reddit, discord and the other sites I used for my extant data attributed a positive emotion relating to video games and their mood. Examples of some of the words used were: “stimulating”, “pleasant change of pace”, “forced but needed staycation” (my favorite), “excellent”, “great” and “definitely helped me” (the most common response). I did notice another pattern, even in those people who said that video games had a positive impact on their quality of life admitted to using them as an escape during the Pandemic, but their feelings about “escaping” were split, where some saw escaping as a negative, poor coping mechanism, and other’s admitted it freely and as “necessary to get away from the crap-hole that is life”. Even in my interview, the interviewee had a negative view of the word “escape” but said he did use games as a way to “take a break”.

After the interview and picking up on some subtle nuances in thought processes that hadn’t occurred to me, I reanalyzed some of the extant data to see if people who overall felt that gaming didn’t help or made things worse, also perceived escaping in a negative way, and 23 of the 34 people who perceived escaping this way, did not feel that video games had a positive impact. Another common thread was peoples use of the words “nothing better to do”, “pass the time” and “it was better than [some other usually undesirable task]”, which suggests that it didn’t necessarily boost those people’s quality of life, but it also didn’t detract from it either.

With the interview, I made a long list of observations, but the most prominent and applicable ones were the use of games as a way to escape, and the positive and negative correlation people associate with the word “escape”, for the interviewee, he clearly saw the word “escape” as a negative, and as I found out later, recognized he did do at the beginning of the pandemic when his gaming was at its peak. He was also one of the few people that saw gaming as both a positive and a negative, though the reason may have been more prevalent in others, but because I was able to have an in-depth interview with him, he was able to differentiate between different time periods in the pandemic and how he viewed gaming at those times. My findings seem relatively in-line with previous studies and seem congruent with what I’ve observed people talking about online and in my personal life as it relates to video games in general.
